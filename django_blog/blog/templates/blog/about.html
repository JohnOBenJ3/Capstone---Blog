About Me

Hi! My name is Orrin Johnson. I like building things. I also like talking about building things. Recently I have become a software engineer but that was not always the case.

For over decade I worked in a custom woodshop making anything from high-end residential cabinets to custom entry systems and furniture. In that time I learned some very foundational things about building stuff. Planning is paramount. Mistakes are inevitable. Some times, it's actually better to just start over. The longer you do something the better you get at it. I've taught quite a few people some fundamental wood-working and the theme is usually the same: getting too far out in front of the task and then trying to repair mistakes in the middle of the process. Or worse, the end. Regardless of how good you get at something, maintaining humility keeps you on your toes because you remember that there is a fine line between success and failure.

Today I've traded the chisels for a keyboard, the mallet for a mouse. Joinery is now through restful routes instead of mortises and tenons. I've become a student all over again, literally. In September of 2019 I joined General Assembly in Denver Colorado to study Web Development and Software Engineering. I had done some self study but felt like I lacked core fundamentals to take me to the next stage. More than once I found myself questioning my decision! This stuff ain't easy, but learning new things usually isn't.

It really wasn't until I started making some connections that things got easier. No, not that I just suddenly became a programming wizard, but rather I was able to take the errors in stride and learn what each mistake was trying to tell me. In carpentry, the mistakes are visible and easier to understand. If there is a gap in your joints you can usually diagnose what you did pretty quickly. In programming on the other hand, you could have any number of countless errors (usually a typo in my case), and you have to sift through some, at times, vague errors to get to the root of the issue. The connection? Carpentry and software engineering actually share some pretty interesting parallels, but at their base level they're both still about one basic thing, building stuff. And I'm all about that.

So the purpose of this blog is to document my journey. The highs. The lows. And I might even throw some wood-working talks in there every now and then. My time at school taught me a lot, about loops and variables and functions and when to use them. More importantly, its taught me how to be a student again. I think when you get pretty good at something you tend to get complacent and that's where the growing stops. I can't take a day off from programming because I'm afraid I'm going to lose some critical information and have to take some steps back. Of course, now that I have some tools in the box, how could I not want to use them!

So thanks for stopping by and checking out my site. If you read something you like or just want to reach out I'd love to hear from you.

That's Bennett. He's a handful.
